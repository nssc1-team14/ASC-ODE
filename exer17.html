Exercise 17 — Mass–Spring Integrators#

Overview#

This exercise documents trajectories for the harmonic oscillator / mass–spring benchmark using explicit Euler, stabilized explicit Euler (square), improved Euler (Heun), implicit Euler, and Crank–Nicolson. The plots below show time histories and phase portraits, allowing comparison of stability and damping for different time steps.

Implicit Euler#

This section sketches the implicit Euler (backward Euler) implementation as outlined in the course notes. The circuit example will be added later; for now we illustrate the method on the harmonic oscillator benchmark.

Phase

The fully implicit update damps the oscillations more strongly than the explicit schemes, providing unconditional stability for this linear test while introducing numerical dissipation.

Crank–Nicolson#

Crank–Nicolson uses the trapezoidal rule (implicit midpoint) for the first-order system version of the mass–spring oscillator, yielding second-order accuracy and only mild numerical damping.

Time

Phase

Compared to implicit Euler, the trajectories stay close to the true ellipse with much less energy loss while still remaining stable for larger time steps than the explicit schemes.

Notes#

  • Explicit Euler shows the expected energy growth and spirals outwards in phase space.

  • The stabilized square scheme reduces drift and keeps oscillations bounded for this step size.

  • Improved Euler provides much better energy conservation and near-elliptic phase trajectories.

  • Implicit Euler is unconditionally stable but introduces significant numerical damping, causing inward spirals in phase space.

  • Crank–Nicolson balances stability and accuracy: it remains stable for larger time steps and largely preserves energy, with slight damping compared to the exact solution.

Comparison across time steps#

  • For coarse time steps, explicit Euler quickly blows up; stabilized explicit Euler holds but distorts amplitude; improved Euler stays reasonable but can drift.

  • Implicit Euler remains stable for any tested step size but heavily damps the motion.

  • Crank–Nicolson stays stable and keeps near-elliptic orbits even with larger steps, making it preferable when both stability and energy behavior matter.
